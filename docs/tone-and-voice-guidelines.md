# Tone of Voice Guidelines

**Ένωση Ελλήνων Ολλανδίας & Το Ελληνικό Σπίτι στο Ρότερνταμ**

# Introduction

The Greek House and the Association of Greeks in the Netherlands are one of the longest-standing and most official Greek presences in the Netherlands.
As a cultural home for the Greek community, our communication should reflect both our history and our living presence today. When sharing an announcement, promoting an event, or communicating online, we want people to feel welcome, informed, connected, and encouraged to participate.
In short, our communication should feel:
- Friendly and approachable
- Fresh and contemporary
- Culturally grounded
- Informative
- Inviting
We communicate with warmth and personality, while remaining clear, trustworthy, appropriate and respectful of the role of the organisation.

# The difference between voice and tone

Our voice is who we are, our identity and position as an organisation, the Greek Association in the Netherlands and, through it, the Greek House in Rotterdam, the cultural entity of it; our tone is how we sound and communicate our values, activities, and community.
The voice of The Greek House and the Association of Greeks in the Netherlands reflects:
- a cultural institution
- a community space
- a historical Greek presence in the Netherlands
- and an organisation that connects generations of Greeks abroad
This is our positioning and the constant that guides how we communicate with our members, community, and audience. Our tone, however, adapts depending on the situation and platform. For example, a celebration can feel energetic and playful. A workshop can feel warm and encouraging. An official announcement can feel calmer, straightforward, and thus, more formal. Overall, the identity stays the same, but the tone adapts to the context.

# Our voice in practice

Our voice reflects the role of The Greek House and the Association of Greeks in the Netherlands as the go-to organisation and trusted cultural presence for the Greek community in the Netherlands.
We communicate as both the Greek House in Rotterdam and the Association of Greeks in the Netherlands.
Our identity includes talking from the point of view of:
- a cultural institution
- a community space
- a historical Greek presence in the Netherlands
- and an organisation that connects generations of Greeks abroad
Our communication should feel open, human, and connected to people’s everyday lives mainly through keeping them engaged and informed, carrying the credibility and responsibility of an established organisation.
We honour Greek culture, history, language, and community, but we communicate in a way that feels lively and relevant today avoiding being distant or institutional.

# Our tone in practice

## Friendly and approachable

We write like people; we are not speaking in a corporate or overly institutional way.
Our communication should feel warm, welcoming, and natural. We avoid overly formal or bureaucratic language.

## Fresh and contemporary

We are a historical organisation, but our communication should feel current and alive.
We avoid stereotypical language and outdated phrasing. Conversational language and emojis are welcome when they feel natural and appropriate.

## Culturally grounded

Greek culture is at the centre of what we do. Our communication should feel authentically Greek: warm, expressive, social, and familiar, while remaining inclusive and accessible to everyone.

## Informative

Even when the tone is playful or enthusiastic, information should always stay clear.
People should immediately understand what is happening, where, when, and how they can participate. Excitement should not make communication confusing.

## Inviting

Our communication should encourage participation and community. We want people to feel included and welcome, whether they already know the organisation or are discovering it for the first time. We want to be their point of reference for all things Greek in the Netherlands and to give them the space to actively approach us with ideas and participation.

# Style, grammar, and mechanics

## Keep it simple

Use clear language and short sentences.
Avoid:
- complicated wording
- corporate language
- unnecessary formalities

## Use active language

Prefer direct communication. It is easier to read and understand, while it is better in convincing the audience to take action and participate in our activities.
Instead of:
- “Η συμμετοχή μπορεί να πραγματοποιηθεί μέσω...”
Prefer:
- “Δηλώστε συμμετοχή μέσω...”

## Emojis

Emojis are welcome when they add warmth or help structure a post. Use them naturally and in moderation.
Avoid too many emojis together, decorative overuse or emojis in serious announcements

## Exclamation marks and capitals

We are expressive, but not chaotic.
Avoid:
- TOO MANY CAPITAL LETTERS
- multiple exclamation marks!!!
- overly dramatic wording
Usually, one exclamation mark is enough.

## Formatting, Structure and SEO

Keep posts easy to read. Keep titles short, clear, and engaging. Use short and concise headlines. Important information should always stand out. Links, registration details, dates, locations, and prices should always be easy to find.
Use:
- spacing
- short paragraphs
- bullet points
- clear structure with a title, subtitles, etc.
At the same time, our communication should be easy to find, read, and understand across search engines, social platforms, and AI-based tools. We write for people first, using clear titles, descriptive language, and natural keywords that reflect the actual content of a post or page. Using direct and specific wording also helps our content remain searchable, readable, and easier to process across different digital platforms and technologies.
Our communication should be accessible and easy to engage with for everyone. We use clear language, readable formatting, short paragraphs, and structured information so content is easy to scan and understand across platforms. Important information such as dates, locations, prices, and registration details should always be clearly stated. When applicable, include alt text or short image descriptions, especially for informative or promotional content. It is also better to place important information in the caption or body text in addition to the image itself, so content remains accessible and easy to read on all devices and platforms.

# Translation

Our communication is trilingual in Greek, Dutch and English.
Greek texts can feel warmer, more conversational, and more expressive. We prefer natural Greek over very formal language.
Translated texts should sound natural and international, not necessarily like direct translations from Greek.
Keep translated copy simple, clear and easy to follow. If a Greek phrase does not translate naturally, adapt it instead of translating it word for word.

# Do’s and Don’ts through examples

These indicative examples show the kind of language that fits our communication style and the kind that should be avoided (some might be strict but you get the point). The goal is not to make every post sound indifferent, but to keep communication consistent, clear, and recognisable.

# Keeping it approachable yet appropriate

## Do

- “Σας περιμένουμε με χαρά!”
- “Ελάτε να γιορτάσουμε μαζί”
- “Μείνετε συντονισμένοι για περισσότερες λεπτομέρειες”
- “Θα χαρούμε να σας δούμε εκεί”

## Don’t

- “Η παρουσία σας κρίνεται επιθυμητή”
- “Παρακαλείσθε όπως προσέλθετε”
- “Το κοινό δύναται να συμμετάσχει”
The second examples are in overly formal or distant language.

# Clear and informative

## Do

- “📅 Κυριακή 31 Μαΐου | 15:00–21:00”
- “Δηλώστε συμμετοχή μέσω της φόρμας στο bio”
- “Η συμμετοχή είναι δωρεάν”

## Don’t

- Long paragraphs where practical information gets lost
- Unclear instructions
- Missing dates, times, or locations
Important information should always be easy to find.

# Fresh and contemporary

## Do

- “Ένα ολοήμερο γλέντι ελληνικών προδιαγραφών 😉”
- “Σας περιμένουμε για μια όμορφη βραδιά”
- “Κλείστε τα εισιτήριά σας εγκαίρως”

## Don’t

- “Μία μεγαλειώδης και ανεπανάληπτος εκδήλωσις”
- “Το απόλυτο event της χρονιάς!!!!”
- Forced internet slang or exaggerated marketing language
We want energy and personality, not abstract hype.

# Emojis and expression

## Do

- “🎨 Εργαστήρι ζωγραφικής για παιδιά”
- “Σας περιμένουμε 🌼”
- “Μείνετε συντονισμένοι 😉”

## Don’t

- “🎉🎉🎉🔥🔥🔥😍😍🇬🇷🇬🇷🇬🇷”
- Using emojis in every sentence
- Emojis in serious announcements or commemorative posts
Emojis should support the message, not overwhelm it.

# Exclamation marks and capitals

## Do

- “Σας περιμένουμε!”
- “Τα εισιτήρια είναι διαθέσιμα”

## Don’t

- “ΣΑΣ ΠΕΡΙΜΕΝΟΥΜΕ ΟΛΟΥΣ!!!!!”
- “ΜΗΝ ΤΟ ΧΑΣΕΤΕ!!!”
Too many capitals and exclamation marks make communication feel unprofessional.

# Translation

## Do

Translate the text into English and Dutch naturally:
- “Join us for a full-day Greek fest”
- “Stay tuned for updates”

## Don’t

Translate word-for-word from Greek:
- “We wait you with joy”
- “Greek feast specifications”
- “Stay tuned from our pages”
English communication should sound natural and easy to read.

# Community feeling

## Do

- “Μαζί γιορτάζουμε…”
- “Η κοινότητά μας”
- “Ελάτε παρέα”
- “Σας περιμένουμε όλους”

## Don’t

- Cold or overly corporate communication
- Language that feels exclusive or distant
- Communication that sounds like advertising instead of community
People should feel like they are being invited into a shared space.
